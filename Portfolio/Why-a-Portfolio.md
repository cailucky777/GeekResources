# Why a Portfolio

You're 15, 16, 17. Why bother?

---

## What a Portfolio Does

- **Proves you can finish things.** Courses prove you can start. Finished projects are rarer and more valuable.
- **Gives you something to talk about.** College essays, interviews, conversations — "I built X" is infinitely better than "I studied Y."
- **Compounds over time.** Every project you ship today is something you still own 5 years from now. Homework evaporates the day it's graded.
- **Creates luck.** People can't offer you opportunities if they don't know what you can do. A portfolio is a magnet.

---

## What Counts

Not every repo needs to be revolutionary. A good portfolio includes:

- **1 "headline" project** — the thing you're most proud of, polished to near-professional quality.
- **2–3 supporting projects** — different domains (a web app, a game, an iOS app), showing range.
- **Side interests** — a blog post, an article, a contribution to an open-source project.

Quality > quantity. Three great projects beat ten half-finished ones.

---

## What Doesn't Count (Much)

- Tutorial clones with zero original ideas. Recruiters recognize them instantly.
- A single commit, README-free project.
- 30 forks with no work of your own.
- Anything you can't demo in 30 seconds.

---

## The First-Impression Stack

A recruiter or interviewer will do, in order:

1. Open your GitHub profile. (**Profile README + top 6 pinned repos.**)
2. Pick the most interesting-looking one.
3. Open its README.
4. Look at a screenshot or GIF.
5. *Maybe* click through the code.

Step 4 is where most students lose. **No screenshot = no interest.**

---

## Student-Friendly Project Ideas

- A small iOS app that solves a real problem in your life (homework tracker, bus schedule, mood journal).
- A visual algorithm — maze solver, pathfinder, sorting visualizer — with a beautiful demo video.
- A content creator project — a newsletter, YouTube channel, blog where you explain what you learned.
- A contribution to an open-source library you actually use.
- A game made in Unity or SpriteKit with a published itch.io page.

---

## How Long Should a Project Take?

Rule of thumb: **scope it to 2–4 weeks of actual work.** Projects that take longer tend to be abandoned. Projects that take less time rarely show enough depth.

If you have an ambitious idea, ship a **v0.1** first — the smallest possible version that does one useful thing — then iterate.

---

## Start Today

You don't need another tutorial. Open a text editor and write:

1. The name of the project.
2. One sentence about what it does.
3. Three features it'll have at v0.1.

Create the GitHub repo. Make the first commit — even if it's just the README.

That's more than most people ever do.
