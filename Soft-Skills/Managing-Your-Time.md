# Managing Your Time

> School + side projects + sleep. You can have all three if you're honest about what each costs.

---

## The Three-Bucket Rule

Every week, reserve time for:

1. **School** — the non-negotiable baseline.
2. **One focused side project** — not five half-started ones.
3. **Rest + people + sport + sleep** — without this, the first two collapse.

If you can't fit all three, something's over-committed.

---

## Consistency Over Intensity

90 minutes of deep work every weekday beats a 14-hour weekend grind. Because:

- Your brain consolidates between sessions.
- You don't burn out.
- Over a year, 90 × 250 = **22,500 minutes** of deep focus. That's a real project.

Pick a time. Make it non-negotiable. Show up even when you don't feel like it.

---

## A Simple Weekly Plan

| Day | Focus |
|---|---|
| Mon | School. Plan the week's side-project task. |
| Tue | 90m side project. |
| Wed | School recovery night (be kind to yourself). |
| Thu | 90m side project. |
| Fri | Small commit, push to GitHub. |
| Sat | Free coding block (2–4 hrs) or rest. Alternate weeks. |
| Sun | Review week, plan next week. |

---

## Break Projects Into Commits

Instead of "finish the app this weekend," make it:

- Monday: Render the main list from hardcoded data.
- Tuesday: Replace with SwiftData.
- Wednesday: Add "create" screen.
- Thursday: Handle empty state.
- Friday: One bug fix + commit + push.

Each day ends with something committed. Momentum from small wins > guilt from big unfinished ones.

---

## Protect Your Sleep

Sleep compiles your code. Seriously — long-term memory forms during sleep. Students routinely trade sleep for studying and *net lose* productivity.

Non-negotiable: **8 hours** on school nights, especially during intense project weeks.

---

## Say No, Politely

You can't join every club, hackathon, and study group. Say yes to two. Say no to the rest, warmly.

"This sounds great — I'm focused on a project this month, but please ask me again next semester" is a perfectly adult thing to say at 16.

---

## When You Fall Behind

Falling behind is normal. The only rule: don't compound it.

1. Stop.
2. Look at the list. Cut 30% ruthlessly.
3. Pick the smallest next step.
4. Do that one thing.
5. Commit. Push. Breathe.

A half-finished side project you ship is worth more than three ambitious ones you abandoned.
